Background and Terminology

Submitted by rick on Fri, 07/21/2017

What disorders are encompassed in this review of depression management?

The course is focused on unipolar depression, which represents the vast majority of depression cases seen by underwriters.

The 2 core disorders are major depressive disorder (MDD) and persistent depressive disorder (PDD).

PDD has elements of chronic MDD and what was called dysthymic disorder in DSM-IV.

These are the most important diagnostic features of the unipolar depression spectrum:

1. MDD requires 4 of 9 symptoms to be present most of the time for at least a 2-week

period:

• Depressed mood

• Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in activities

• Significant weight loss without dieting

• Insomnia or hypersomnia

• Psychomotor agitation or retardation

• Fatigue or loss of energy

• Feelings of worthlessness/hopelessness

• Diminished ability to think/concentrate

• Recurrent thoughts of death, recurrent suicidal ideation or a suicide plan

• In addition, MDD should have a specified severity: mild, moderate, severe or with psychotic features

2. PDD requires a depressive mood for most of the day, most days, for 2 years, plus 2 of 6

additional symptoms akin to MDD.

3. There are additional specifiers that may be used with MDD:

• Recurrent brief depression lasting 2-13 days at least once a month for 12 consecutive months.

• Short duration depressive episode lasting 4-13 days.

• Depressive episode with insufficient symptoms, which requires a depressed affect and at least 1 of the other 8 symptoms to be present for at least 2 weeks. This is the DSM-5 equivalent of subclinical depression.

• Anxious distress, with 2 of 5 anxiety symptoms.

• Mixed features, with 3 of 7 bipolar-type symptoms, but not sufficient to diagnose bipolar disorder.

• Melancholic features, with loss of pleasure in most activities, lack of reactivity to most

 

pleasurable stimuli, plus 3 of 6 additional severe symptoms.

• Atypical features, with mood reactivity and 2 of 4 other symptoms: significant weight gain/increased appetite, hypersomnia, heavy feeling in arms and legs (leaden paralysis) and rejection sensitivity.

• Psychotic features, with delusions and/or hallucinations.

• Peripartum onset, which includes postpartum depression.

• Season pattern, with depressive symptoms during one season of the year and recovery at a characteristic season-related time.

The 2 RED FLAG presentations of MDD are:

• With melancholic features

• With psychotic features

The other 2 main presentations of major depression are:

• Bipolar depression

• Depression in schizoaffective disorder

These presentations are not discussed in this course because they are managed differently than MDD is.

Who treats most patients with depression?

Primary care generalists (73%), followed by psychiatrists and other mental healthcare specialists (psychologists, etc.).

Severe MDD cases are twice as likely to be seen by/referred to psychiatrists. [Olfson-2]

What are the goals of depression treatment? [Culpepper]

• To induce a remission, eliminating as many symptoms and other manifestations as possible

• To prevent a relapse or recurrence

• To help the patient restore the feeling of being in emotional control

• To help the patient return to his usual level of functioning

What are the main treatment options?

• Psychotherapy

• Prescription drugs

• Other medical interventions such as ECT (electroconvulsive therapy)

• Hospitalization (inpatient care)

• Exercise and related therapies

• Alternative and complementary remedies

 

What is the role of psychotherapy in MDD?

It is considered 1st-line treatment.

This said, the treatment guidelines promulgated by the America Psychiatric Association (APA) support immediate antidepressant prescribing at any level of MDD severity.

This suggests that they consider psychotherapy to be less effective.

The American College of Physicians (ACP) advises clinicians to choose between drugs and cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), a type of psychotherapy.

Given how busy primary care physicians are and the limitations on time to see a patient imposed by corporate medicine, it is easy to appreciate why drug treatment is chosen more often than any type of counseling.

In other countries, psychotherapy is seen as playing a much larger 1st-line role. In fact, UK guidelines discourage antidepressant prescribing in milder forms of depression, at least initially.

[Paris, Qaseem-1]

What are the main types of psychotherapy?

There are at least 6 major approaches, the most common being cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT).

The specific type of psychotherapy used has no bearing on insurability. [Qaseem-1]

Is there any significant difference in effectiveness between psychotherapy and antidepres- sant therapy?

No, with one caveat: severe depression generally requires pharmacological treatment added to psychotherapy to achieve the highest probability of remission.

[Amick, Gartlehner-1, Guidi, Hollon, Huhn, Maust, Rayer, Weitz]

 

What are the risk factors for a poor outcome with psychotherapy?

Vittengl reported these 5 RED FLAGS specifically for cognitive behavioral therapy, but they likely apply to all forms of psychotherapy:

• Major psychiatric and/or nonpsychiatric comorbidities

• Suicidality

• Unemployment

• Prior inpatient psychiatric care

• Severe MDD

How much has antidepressant (AD) use increased in the last 15 years?

In the USA, it has increased from 9.1% of the adult population in 2001-2002 to 13% in 2011- 2012.

• Roughly 65% of this is increased use of the selective serotonin reuptake inhibitor (SSRI) class of antidepressants.

• Antidepressants are now the most commonly prescribed drugs in the USA.

• Depression patients seek out medication sooner than persons with other psychiatric impairments.

[Del Valle, Kantor, Mojtabai, Olfson-2]

What is the #1 factor determining whether AD medication is more effective than placebo?

Depression severity.

ADs tend to be most effective in severe depression, whereas their therapeutic impact is marginal in mild-to-moderate depression.

A placebo is a sham “medication” with no active ingredients. Placebos are used in pharmaceutical evaluation programs, called clinical trials, to gauge the drug’s impact.

Surprising as it may seem, the response rate to placebos in subjects with mild and moderate depression is 35% and this as good as the response to 1st line antidepressants in most community-based studies.

[Chekroud, Fournier, Furukawa, Ioannidis, Paris, Tedeschini, Van der Lem]

Do experts favor antidepressant use for managing subclinical depression (DSM-5 term: de- pressive episode with insufficient symptoms)?

 

Some do.

And those favoring AD use in subclinical depression usually do so only when that depression exerts a significantly adverse functional impact. These cases of subclinical depression have the same underwriting implications as major depressive disorder.

[Kroenke, Santarsieri]

How is MDD treated in patients with a comorbid anxiety disorder?

Typically with a benzodiazepine, but only until the AD drug starts to exert an effect. This usually takes 3-4 weeks after initiation of treatment.

SSRIs are deemed effective in treating anxiety disorders, making ongoing use of benzodiazepines or other drugs for anxiety symptoms unnecessary.

If a benzodiazepine continues to be prescribed, we need to know why. The most common reason is insomnia.

The exception to this rule is the use of alprazolam to treat atypical depression.

[Carrier, Coplan, Gaspersz]

Do underwriters sometimes overreact to antidepressant use?

Yes.

“...treatments have become more and more pharmacological and patients with the mildest disorders are likely to have the highest placebo response rate. Once recovered, they misattribute their good improvement to an unnecessary medication and stay on it for prolonged periods. Overuse of psychotropic drugs has become a serious threat to public health.”

Laura Batstra, PhD, and Allen Franen, MD Duke University Medical School Journal of Mental and Nervous Disease 200(2012):474

In audits, we have seen underwriters add excessive debits for mild cases of depression because they were treated with 1st-line antidepressants.

Bottom line: the fact that an applicant was or is being treated with an AD does not, by itself, confer increased mortality risk .

 

Most applicants treated medically for mood symptoms described as “depression” have no excess mortality on that basis .

What other depression interventions does the American Psychiatric Association recom- mend?

• ECT

• Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS)

• Phototherapy (light therapy)

These are recommended for patients who are resistant to treatment with medication and psychotherapy (more on this later).

[Chen, Gaynes-2, Lam-1]

Is light therapy effective?

Yes.

Although originally used only for seasonal major depression, it has also been shown to be helpful in nonseasonal cases. [Güzel Özdemir, Lam-2, Perera]

What do underwriters need to know about electroconvulsive therapy (ECT)?

• It is safe and effective in acute treatment of Rx-resistant depression at all adult ages.

• It is usually done in severe depression cases managed as inpatients.

• The risk of post-ECT treatment relapse is high, and maintenance treatment is often needed, ranging from weekly to monthly.

• Coronary artery disease patients may experience ischemic episodes during ECT.

• There are also adverse cognitive side effects in some cases, and this has served to limit the use of ECT in elderly patients.

Use of ECT is always a RED FLAG in underwriting .

[Kellner, Tess, Unützer, van Schaik]

What other RED FLAG non-drug interventions are used in depression?

• Whole body hyperthermia [Janssen]

• Deep brain stimulation with surgically implanted intracerebral electrodes [Temel]

 

What other intervention is as effective as psychotherapy or Rx in many patients with depres- sion?

Mild-to-moderate intensity aerobic exercise, as well as other exercise/physical activity programs, including tai chi and yoga.

[Blumenthal, Cooney, Gartelhner-1, Prathikanti]

Key Take-Away Points:

• The 2 core depressive disorders in DSM-5 are major depressive disorder (MDD) and persistent depressive disorder (PDD).

• PDD has elements of MDD and DSM-IV dysthymic disorder.

• Underwriters should know the criteria for MDD and PDD.

• There are additional specifiers used to identify distinct subsets of depression cases. Underwriters should be broadly familiar with them.

• The RED FLAG presentations are depression with melancholic features or with psychotic features.

• Primary care doctors treat most depression cases.

• Psychotherapy is a 1st-line treatment for MDD.

• There is no significant difference in effectiveness between psychotherapy and antidepressant therapy.

• The #1 determinant of antidepressant effectiveness is depression severity. ADs are highly effective in severe depression, but their impact is marginal in mild-to-moderate depression.

• Benzodiazepines are commonly used for the first 3-4 weeks when an anxiety disorder is comorbid with MDD. Once AD therapy begins to be effective, benzodiazepine therapy should end unless needed for insomnia.

• The main exception here is alprazolam for atypical depression.

• The fact that an applicant was or is being treated with an AD does not, by itself, confer increased mortality risk.

• The 3 MDD treatments recommended by the American Psychiatric Association are: electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) and phototherapy (light therapy).

• ECT is safe and effective, but constitutes a RED FLAG because it is used in severe Rx-resistant cases only.

• Minimal-to-moderate intensity aerobic exercise and other exercise/physical activity programs (including yoga and tai chi) are also effective in mild-to-moderate MDD.
